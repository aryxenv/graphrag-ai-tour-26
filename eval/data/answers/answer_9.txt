Overview of Dick Wilkins
Dick Wilkins is a character from Charles Dickens' "A Christmas Carol," appearing in the memories of Ebenezer Scrooge as witnessed during the visit of the Ghost of Christmas Past. He is most notably recognized as Scrooge’s fellow apprentice during their youth when both worked under the kindly Mr. Fezziwig [Data: Entities (22, 195, 196)].

Connection to Scrooge
Dick Wilkins and Scrooge shared a close companionship during their apprenticeship at Fezziwig’s warehouse. They are depicted as enthusiastic and cheerful young men who deeply appreciated Fezziwig’s generosity and festive spirit, especially during the memorable Christmas Eve parties organized by their employer. The text describes Dick as being "very much attached" to Scrooge, highlighting a bond of friendship and camaraderie from a happier, more innocent period in Scrooge’s life [Data: Entities (22, 195, 196); Sources (14)].

During these festive occasions, Dick and Scrooge worked together to help prepare the warehouse for dancing and celebrations, demonstrating teamwork and youthful energy. Their positive experiences under Fezziwig’s mentorship serve as a poignant contrast to Scrooge’s later miserly and isolated demeanor, underscoring the transformative impact of kindness and community in his formative years [Data: Entities (22, 195, 196); Sources (14, 15)].

Commentary
The relationship between Dick Wilkins and Scrooge serves to humanize Scrooge by reminding him—and the reader—of the joy he once found in friendship and communal happiness. This friendship is an important part of Scrooge's reflection and eventual transformation, as it embodies the warmth and generosity that he later learns to embrace again. Dick Wilkins, though a relatively minor character, plays a significant role in illustrating the themes of lost innocence and the enduring value of compassion [Data: Entities (22, 195, 196); Sources (14)].

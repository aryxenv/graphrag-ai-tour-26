Comparing Perceptions of Scrooge: Fred’s Party vs. the Businessmen’s Discussion
Scrooge at Fred’s Party
At Fred’s Christmas party, Scrooge is the subject of a lively guessing game and good-natured mockery. Fred and his guests describe Scrooge as a “disagreeable animal” and laugh at his miserly, grumpy ways. However, the tone is affectionate rather than cruel. Fred, in particular, insists on toasting to his uncle’s health, wishing him a merry Christmas and a happy New Year, and expressing hope for his redemption. He tells his guests, “I am sorry for him; I couldn’t be angry with him if I tried. Who suffers by his ill whims? Himself always... I mean to give him the same chance every year, whether he likes it or not, for I pity him” [Data: Sources (25, 26, 27+more)]. The partygoers’ laughter and games show that, while they find Scrooge’s ways ridiculous, they do not hate him. Instead, they view him with a mix of amusement, exasperation, and, in Fred’s case, genuine compassion and hope for his change [Data: Sources (27)].

Scrooge at the Businessmen’s Discussion
In stark contrast, the businessmen’s discussion of Scrooge’s death is marked by indifference, self-interest, and even a touch of dark humor. They speak of his passing with little emotion, focusing on practical matters such as who will attend his funeral and what has become of his money. One man jokes, “I don’t mind going if a lunch is provided,” while another speculates that Scrooge may have left his wealth to his company, but notes, “He hasn’t left it to me. That’s all I know.” Their laughter and lack of genuine mourning highlight how Scrooge, despite his riches, failed to form meaningful connections or earn the respect of his peers [Data: Sources (29)]. The businessmen’s remarks are transactional and impersonal, devoid of warmth or hope, and reflect a society that values wealth over human connection.

What the Contrast Reveals
This contrast reveals a central moral lesson of A Christmas Carol: one’s legacy is shaped not by wealth or status, but by kindness, empathy, and the willingness to connect with others. At Fred’s party, even criticism is softened by familial care and optimism for Scrooge’s redemption. The warmth, humor, and lingering hope at Fred’s festivities underscore the possibility of personal transformation and forgiveness. In contrast, the businessmen’s conversation exposes the emptiness of a life defined solely by commerce and self-interest, where social ties are shallow and measured purely by utility [Data: Sources (29)].

Dickens uses this juxtaposition to critique Victorian materialism and to highlight the transformative potential of empathy and acceptance. The affection and hope at Fred’s party offer Scrooge a path to redemption, while the businessmen’s indifference serves as a warning of the social oblivion that awaits those who prioritize money over human relationships. Ultimately, the contrast underscores the novella’s message: true wealth lies in generosity, compassion, and the bonds we form with others [Data: Sources (25, 26, 27, 29)].

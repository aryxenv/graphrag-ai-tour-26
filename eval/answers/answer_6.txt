Marley's Ghost: Appearance and Attire
When Marley's Ghost appears to Ebenezer Scrooge, his haunted visage is marked by the remnants of his mortal identity. He is described as wearing the same sort of clothes he wore in life: he retains his pigtail, waistcoat, tights, and boots. Notably, his ghostly form preserves his appearance "exactly as he had in life," underscoring the connection between who Marley was and the fate he suffers after death [Data: Entities (29, 40); Relationships (40, 182)].

The Chain: Its Composition and Symbolism
Marley's most distinctive and symbolic feature as a ghost is the "ponderous chain" he drags. The chain is not a simple iron shackle but rather an intricate construction made from objects representing his materialistic and selfish life. Specifically, the chain is described as being "made of cash-boxes, keys, padlocks, ledgers, deeds, and heavy purses wrought in steel." Each link represents an aspect of Marley's business-focused existence, serving as a visible emblem of his spiritual burden and regret for neglecting charity and humanity while alive [Data: Entities (29, 40, 118); Sources (8)].

Commentary
The explicit enumeration of items in Marley's chain not only illustrates his lifelong obsession with money and business but also functions as a moral warning for Scrooge (and the reader) about the consequences of a life led without compassion or generosity. The vivid imagery of Marley's attire and chains helps establish the eerie and cautionary tone of his supernatural visitation, forming a pivotal moment in the narrative and in Scrooge's journey toward redemption [Data: Entities (29, 40, 118); Relationships (41, 151, 181)].

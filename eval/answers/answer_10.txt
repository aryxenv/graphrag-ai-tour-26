At Fred’s Christmas party, one of the main games played is a lively round of "Yes and No," a classic Victorian guessing game. In this game, Fred thinks of something and the other guests must deduce what it is by asking only yes-or-no questions. The particular instance recorded in the story involves Fred thinking of "an animal, a live animal, rather a disagreeable animal, a savage animal" and so forth, as his guests try to guess the answer with increasing hilarity [Data: Reports (13); Entities (19, 318, 324); Relationships (524, 527, 532); Sources (27)].

After much laughter and guessing, it is the plump sister who exclaims the answer—"It's your uncle Scro-o-o-o-oge." Thus, the subject of the riddle is none other than Scrooge himself, humorously described through the game as a grumpy, solitary creature, to the amusement of all present. This playful scene not only showcases the joyful spirit and camaraderie of the party, but also gently pokes fun at Scrooge's notorious disposition [Data: Entities (324, 60); Relationships (558, 556); Sources (27)].

The resolution of the game concludes with the party drinking a toast to "Uncle Scrooge," reinforcing both their goodwill and their hopeful, forgiving approach toward even difficult family members—a central theme of the gathering and the novel overall [Data: Relationships (578); Sources (27)].
